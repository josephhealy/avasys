• COTS VS. CUSTOM

    • COTS is always the way to go when requirements are common within major market sectors. However, vendors' COTS claims become increasingly disingenuous, if not cynical, when requirements are specialized to the point they demand high levels of customization. Such specialized requirements tend to drive creation of niche software markets and at a certain 'niche' threshold most all 'COTS' software today is anything but. Most such vendors make the majority of their revenue consulting to deliver custom, one-off installs of their 'product'. Don't be fooled.

  • WRITE IT OURSELVES?

    • You thoroughly vetted commercial and Open Source software, but your unique requirements coupled with a high level of intellectual property just weren't a fit, so your project got a green light. You got a 'go', but is it the best use of your resources? It may be, but you also have a dizzying array of resources from contractors to offshoring houses at your disposal. In-house, outsource, offshore or hybrid resources? The answer typically turns on a combination of IP, availability of domain experts, and the adequacy of requisite technical skills.

  • AGILE OR WATERFALL?

    • Agile and her debutante sister 'Lean' are the current belles of the ball. These methodologies are designed to tear down walls between project stakeholders, operate in small teams without detailed upfront requirements, and focus on continuous delivery. 'Waterfall' approaches focus on 'Big Design Up Front', tend to have larger, more structured teams, and many 'checks and balances'. While agile / lean are very popular, waterfall still has a place for some very large scale, monolithic, and / or life-critical applications such as avionics, medical devices, air traffic control, and taxation.

  • IS BIG REALLY BETTER?

    • Team size is a much belabored question. The answer is typically driven by how self-contained teams are, their level of interaction, and proposed development methodology. Traditional 'waterfall' approaches are usually hierarchically structured with strict interactions between various stakeholders and tend to be comprised of larger, often distributed, teams. 'Agile / Lean' approaches on the other hand are far less structured and stakeholders are more 'intimately' involved on a day-to-day basis so teams tend to be more compact to avoid them becoming unwieldy.

  • SHOULD WE OFFSHORE?

    • The bottom line? Offshoring won't up your odds of success, but failing is a whole lot cheaper. The question is does it up your risk? What's it take to work offshore with a high level of confidence? Short answer - your subject matter experts have to be highly available, your technical oversight exceptionally talented and patient, and someone has to spend regular (monthly?) face-to-face time with the offshore team in-person or video conferencing. One caveat: it's all but impossible to map Agile / Lean methodologies offshore despite insistent claims to the contrary.

  • HOW CODE STACKS UP

    • LAMP, J2EE and .NET - oh my!!! Is one really 'better' than the others? Depends - and these aren't the only players with Python, Perl, Ruby, and Flex up there too. Even lowly javascript is poised to become a major player in an HTML5 world. So which to pick? Hmmm, it depends on a number of factors like what available resources know, what kind of application (web, mobile, desktop, product, service, cloud), deployment infrastructure, etc. There is no 'simple' answer and all these factors should be considered as you plan and vet a project.

  • WHAT ARCHITECTURE?

    • Our notions of architecture and design have changed substantially over the past five years. Early Smalltalk patterns have been rediscovered and new service-oriented ones have evolved along with varying degrees of associated tooling. The web, mobile devices, and large-scale social media apps have forced many redefinitions of commonly held ideas, beliefs and assumptions. The good news is that, by and large, most of the current themes in architecture and design patterns are being expressed in the various technology stacks commonly in use today.

  • A CLOUDY FORECAST?

    • Cloud computing makes a whole lot of sense for certain classes of applications. And we would be seeing a headlong rush into the clouds except for the fact the technology is running a bit ahead of privacy and security concerns, as well as the ability of our laws and government to deal with the many issues raised by applications and data without borders. Many of these issues will be managed by the development of cloud / premise hybrids where your compute cycles will come from the cloud, but your data will be served from your premises. Be sure and stay tuned to the forecast!

  • LET'S GET SAASY!!!

    • 'Software as a Service' (SaaS) is a fairly familiar term these days describing hosting of software in the cloud, less so are the 'Platform' (PaaS) and 'Infrastructure' (IaaS) versions of the base concept. 'IaaS' can be thought of as leasing datacenter resources, only they are provisioned via the cloud - Amazon's EC2 is a good example of this. 'PaaS' is best thought of as cloud-based software development and deployment environments - Salesforce.com's Force.com, Google's AppEngine, and Microsoft's Azure all fall into this category.

  • MULTITENANT QUANDRY

    • How do companies 'share' applications in the cloud? What if we want different screens? Is their data all mixed in with ours! These issues are being raised about cloud apps and it's forcing the revisiting of old issues from the early days of 'computer time sharing'. 'Virtual machines' (VMs) providing each customer with their own copy of an application and database are a cheap faux-SaaS today because true 'SaaS' multitenant applications are still quite complex and challenging to build. Know the differences between them.

  • WHAT IS NOSQL?

    • NoSQL is a recent, but quiet, revolution in databases driven by the startling data requirements of popular social media applications such as Facebook, LinkedIn, Blogger, Twitter, Wikipedia, Flickr, et al. These high-volume demands began forcing fundamental tradeoffs between performance and the 'ACID' guarantees of relational databases. NoSQL databases tend to be highly distributed, heavily 'sharded', provide only 'eventual consistency', and have limited query options. They're the Young Turks of the database world.

  • C-LEVEL COMMITMENT

    • No software project should ever be undertaken without solid board, c-level, or leadership team sponsorship, commitment and support. The need for such [public] sponsorship can't be underestimated where promoting a new software project is concerned. Sponsorship drives at the heart of the idea technology investments should increase an organization's effectiveness or efficiency and the priorities of each are driven by the leadership team and a coherent business strategy.

  • ADEQUATE FUNDING

    • This speaks for itself and to the commitment by your leadership team. If you can't secure adequate funding for your software project then you're better off not starting it until you have. Without adequate funding you won't be able to properly resource and provision a project and that will ultimately lead to a lingering downward spiral of death marches, missed deliverables, and unhappy clients and end users.

  • APPROPRIATE STAFFING

    • There is no substitute for appropriate technical resources. Without seasoned pros to mentor younger developers they will make costly guesses and gambles again and again. Technical oversight will lapse and wrong turns won't be detected until they are very expensive to correct. Enthusiasm and intent have to be accompanied by the smart application of design patterns, effective tool use, and a good understanding of the technical options available at any point in the project. Your team shouldn't be googling for what to do next or how to do it at every turn.

  • DOMAIN EXPERTISE

    • Domain expertise is what your 'subject matter' experts wield - hopefully in spades. BUT, they have to have more to be useful to a software project - they have to be available. This is very important in any project, but rises to an absolute imperative on outsourced and offshore projects. If your domain experts don't invest in, or can't devote enough time to, your project then the developers are going to be doing a lot of guessing and many of those guesses will be wrong. Appropriate and adequate domain expertise is essential.
